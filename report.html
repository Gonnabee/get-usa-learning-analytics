01 — Problem Definition & Analytical Objectives

How Is Learning on a Field Study Measured?

In a critical museology field study that asks "what is exhibited and what is concealed," we transform the 750 records left by 24 students into learning analytics.

Context — "The Seen and The Unseen"

This dataset records the Taejae University GET USA study tour (2026.03.29–04.02, New York · Philadelphia · Washington DC). The tour is a process of critically reading what the United States exhibits (the seen) and what it conceals (the unseen) through space, architecture, and display; the handout assigns explicit learning objectives to each site. We coded these objectives into five themes.

ThemeRepresentative SiteCore Question (Handout)
O1 The Logic of SpaceMet CloistersIs it a museum of medieval Europe, or an American museum that aestheticizes the Middle Ages?
O2 Objects and ClassificationThe Met · MoMAWhat is classified as 'art' and what as 'civilization/artifact'?
O3 Public Memory and RitualIndependence Hall · LincolnHow do founding and national symbols become ritual?
O4 Revealing and ConcealingNMAAHC · American Indian · HolocaustWhich histories are made 'impossible to ignore'?
O5 Authority and Cultural PowerUS Capitol · Library of CongressOn the walls of the national library, who is left out?

The Problem — The Invisibility of Assessment

Experiential field learning has great educational value, but it is difficult to assess. Unlike the classroom, there is no in-the-moment exam or deliverable, and the instructor cannot simultaneously observe what 24 students attended to across 11 sites. The retrospective essay is a post hoc reconstruction that distorts attention as it was in the moment. All three parties carry a blind spot.

The Instructor's Blind Spot
There is no way to know whether students actually attended to the learning objectives of each site.
The Peers' Blind Spot
With no space to see the thinking of peers and seniors, learning is dispersed privately.
The Individual's Blind Spot
There is no tool to analyze for oneself "where my gaze lingered."

The Contribution of Data Analysis

FieldAtlas captures memos, photos, location, and time in the moment on site. This is not a retrospection but an unobtrusive behavioral trace, revealing what a student actually paused before more directly than an essay does. The analysis (1) quantifies this trace into measurable engagement, (2) clusters its meaning, and (3) tests it statistically to make it visible to all three parties.

Research Questions (RQ)

RQQuestionMethod
RQ1 · DistributionWhen, where, and how much was attention concentrated?Daily/hourly/weekday distributions
RQ2 · DepthWhich encounters produced deep reflection rather than a snapshot?Memo length distribution · semantic clusters
RQ3 · AlignmentDid recorded attention match the site learning objectives?Objective mapping · coverage
RQ4 · DifferenceDid sites elicit different kinds of engagement, and is that difference significant?MANOVA · Chi-square
Thesis

Using on-site behavioral traces rather than retrospection, we provide the instructor with evidence of objective attainment, peers with a shared interpretive space, and the individual with a mirror of their own attention.

05 — Insights & Analytical Findings

Did Attention Flow as Taught?

The core result for RQ3: recorded attention was substantially aligned with the intended learning objectives, and the most affective objective drew the greatest engagement. Yet one objective clearly fell short.

Learning-Objective Coverage
Distribution of 426 memo→objective mappings

The Strongest Objective Drew the Greatest Attention

The course-title theme O4 Revealing and Concealing (NMAAHC · Holocaust · American Indian) was the highest at 136 records (32%). The highest-engagement site (NMAAHC, 80 records) was also the site of the deepest reflection.

Underperforming Objective — Instructor Flag

O5 Authority and Cultural Power (Capitol · Library of Congress) was the lowest at 37 records (9%). The objective of "who is left off the walls" was relatively less engaged.

Not uniform — O4 Revealing over-represented (136), O5 Authority under-represented (37).
⚠ Limitation Memos cluster within student and place (pseudoreplication), and some cells have expected frequency < 5. p-values are secondary — effect sizes (V, w) and the visual patterns are the primary evidence.

A non-significant MANOVA is not a failure but a finding — sites are different 'topics,' not different 'recording structures.' Volume and topic (V=0.56–0.71, large effect) divide the cities, whereas the photo–memo relationship does not. Because of non-independence and sparse cells, we read p conservatively and treat effect size as the primary evidence.

06 — Recommendations & Decision Support

Not a Pretty Picture, but a Decision Tool

The value of analysis is realized when it changes someone's decision. This dashboard is a decision tool that fills the blind spots of all three parties.

Decision Support for the Three Parties

Instructor
Objective-alignment dashboard. Reinforce the low-engagement objective (O5 Authority/Power, 9%) with an advance prompt on the next tour. Adjust the weight of high-engagement content (O4).
Peers · Seniors
The semantic map = a shared interpretive space. Explore the reflections of peers and seniors by meaning, turning private learning into a collective asset.
Individual
A personal attention trajectory. Retrace "where my gaze lingered" by site, time, and language for metacognition.

Practical Recommendations

  • Morning concentration (49%) → schedule core learning activities and discussions in the morning hours.
  • O5 shortfall (9%) → provide a "who is left out" questionnaire in advance of visiting the Capitol and Library of Congress.
  • Convergence at the tour's end → reexamine the length and density of the later itinerary.
  • Intent vs. actual loop → compare objective weight (syllabus) ↔ actual record weight each term to adjust the curriculum.
The Decision in One Line

"The intent was X and the actual was Y, and the evidence is these memos" — design the next tour on an evidentiary basis.

07 — Reflection, Limitations & Future

What Can Be Trusted, and What Cannot

Acknowledging limitations first strengthens the credibility of the analysis.

Limitations

CategoryLimitationImplication
Unit of aggregationAggregated across 24 students, skewed by 1 person with 404 memosIndividual/peer drill-down incomplete → first-class user_id filtering needed
Measurement validityPhoto/memo counts are only an engagement proxy'attended to' ≠ 'learned' · supplement with qualitative evidence
MappingObjective mapping is a handout-based heuristic (426/750)illustrative — the 324 unmapped records are everyday life/transit
Statistical assumptionsMemos are non-independent (pseudoreplication) · some cells have expected frequency <5 · MANOVA normality untestedp is conservative; effect sizes (Cramér's V · Cohen's w) are the primary evidence
FeaturesLanguage detection, city mapping, and topic classification are rule-basedShort memos may be misclassified
SampleSingle cohort · single tourSuited to diagnosing this tour rather than to generalization

Future Improvements

  • Separate three-party views — individual/peer/instructor modes, with first-class user_id filtering.
  • Automate objective alignment — code the site learning objectives and auto-match them to embedding clusters, auto-flagging underperforming objectives.
  • Peer sharing space — accumulate the semantic map across senior cohorts to build continuity of interpretation.
  • Measurement validation — model the argumentative structure and critical-thinking signals of memos to refine 'depth.'
